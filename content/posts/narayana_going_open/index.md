For a long time, the core operations of Narayana have been heavily reliant on internal Red Hat infrastructure, keeping our complex build processes and detailed testing environments somewhat insulated from the broader developer community. While this operational setup served our internal teams well for years, providing a highly stable environment for developing a rigorous distributed transaction manager, it inadvertently created a structural barrier for external contributors. When a project builds its core feedback loops behind a corporate firewall, the resulting opacity limits how effectively the wider community can participate in the engineering process. We deeply realise that for our open-source project to truly thrive, its underlying operational mechanics must be as open, accessible, and transparent as its source code. To better support our developer community and align with modern collaborative practices, we are moving our day-to-day operations out from behind the VPN and onto public, universally accessible platforms. For now, the Narayana team is focusing our efforts on three major infrastructural transitions: migrating our continuous integration pipelines, transitioning our issue tracking system, and fully automating our release workflow.

## Migrating CI to GitHub Actions

The first major step in this structural transition, which we are pleased to share is already well underway, involves fundamentally relocating our continuous integration and delivery pipelines. Historically, our extensive test suites ran on internal Jenkins instances. Building and validating a distributed transaction manager inherently requires rigorous testing to ensure absolute data integrity under complex failure modes, and our internal setup handled this computational lifting. However, this architecture meant that community members submitting pull requests could not directly see why a specific build failed. They were forced to rely on a core maintainer to manually proxy the execution logs, explain the nature of the failure, and subsequently trigger rebuilds. This asynchronous, human-in-the-loop proxying was an anti-pattern for efficient collaboration. To resolve this bottleneck, we have now successfully migrated the vast majority of our Jenkins pipelines over to GitHub Actions. This architectural shift brings our testing surface into the light, removing the need for maintainer intervention just to read a basic stack trace.

By moving to GitHub Actions, we now provide everyone with full visibility into the daily build results on our main development branch, as well as the detailed test executions for all incoming pull requests. When you submit a patch today, you can immediately monitor the test runners, inspect the environment state, and see exactly which transactional edge case your code might have accidentally tripped up. Exposing this telemetry directly within the pull request interface drastically reduces the inherent friction of contributing to a complex system. It empowers developers to iterate on their patches independently (such as within their own fork of Narayana) creating a tighter, more efficient feedback loop. Testing is the bedrock of a critical project like Narayana, and by making our continuous integration publicly visible, we are sharing the responsibility of ensuring systemic reliability with the entire contributing community.

## Moving Issue Tracking to GitHub Issues

Alongside making our testing infrastructure transparent, we are fundamentally restructuring how we track software bugs, manage technical debt, and plan feature requests by moving away from JIRA and entirely embracing GitHub Issues. For many years, having our source code residing in one repository and our engineering conversations taking place in a completely separate issue tracker fragmented the contributor experience. This separation required users to manage separate accounts, manually cross-reference links between platforms, and endure a high degree of mental context switching just to connect a specific line of code to its originating bug report. Recognising that bringing the operational context directly to the exact place where the code lives is critical for lowering the barrier to entry, we are actively moving our entire issue tracking apparatus over to GitHub. All currently open JIRA tickets have been systematically migrated to GitHub Issues, preserving their immediate relevance.

During this extensive data migration, we were careful not to lose the rich historical context that is profoundly vital to the ongoing maintenance of a long-running middleware project. We have intentionally retained the old JIRA system as a strictly read-only archive. This decision ensures that years of historical design decisions, architectural debates, and legacy bug reports remain fully accessible for deep technical reference whenever required. However, moving the raw data is only the first part of solving the issue tracking equation. The inherent complexity of a legacy JIRA instance often lies in its myriad custom issue states, nested project components, and rigidly enforced permission schemes. In our new, streamlined GitHub environment, we are actively stripping away that unnecessary bureaucratic complexity. Our core focus is now on intelligently mapping those legacy workflow concepts into a much flatter, far more accessible taxonomy utilising GitHub labels, ensuring that the metadata ultimately serves the developer.

## Standardising Issue Workflows

To make contributing as remarkably seamless as possible moving forward, our immediate next steps involve carefully standardising these newly adopted issue workflows. We are currently defining clear, concise guidelines for opening new issues, ensuring that both bug reports and feature requests capture the necessary technical context without burdening the reporter with overly bureaucratic data entry templates. Furthermore, we are establishing a unified labelling system to accurately track the lifecycle of a task from its initial proposal, through the triage phase, into active development, and finally through integration. To complement this effort, we are designing a public Kanban board situated directly within the GitHub project interface. This board will serve as a dynamic, widely shared window into our strategic engineering priorities. Instead of continuously wondering what the core team is planning for the next release, the community will be able to easily see our active work streams, the prioritised backlog of issues, and exactly where community help is most needed. It effectively transforms project management from a closed-door meeting into a publicly observable state machine.

## Automating the Release Workflow

The final technical pillar of this sweeping operational transition is the complete automation of our release workflow. Historically, successfully cutting a new release of Narayana involved a specific series of manual steps directly tied to internal infrastructure and local developer environments, which heavily obscured the final stage of the software delivery lifecycle. Manual software releases are inherently fragile, prone to human error, and exceedingly difficult to independently audit. To resolve this, we are defining and systematically implementing a dedicated GitHub Actions workflow designed specifically to automate the release of Narayana artefacts directly to Maven Central. By scripting and publicising this entire deployment pipeline, we completely eliminate the historical opacity surrounding exactly how our release binaries are minted, cryptographically signed, and globally published. This technical implementation ensures that the entire lifecycle of a community contribution, from the initial issue creation through the code review process to the final artefact distribution, can be fully audited and clearly understood by absolutely anyone in the developer community. It completely removes the perceived magic from the release process, systematically replacing it with deterministic, open infrastructure.

## Join Us on This Journey

We view these profound infrastructural changes not merely as a simple operational upgrade or a shift in tooling, but as a deeply collaborative, continuously evolving journey undertaken alongside our developer community. Bringing our core technical operations into the open is a structural effort to effectively empower everyone to co-develop and collectively shape the project together on a completely equal footing. By actively dismantling the legacy walls surrounding our continuous integration systems, our issue tracking databases, and our release engineering pipelines, we are actively distributing the ownership of the project's overall health and its future direction. As we navigate these three major transitions, we welcome your pragmatic feedback and technical insights to help us carefully fine-tune the direction of this ongoing effort. Our ultimate goal is to ensure that the future of Narayana is built openly by, and functionally for, the very community that sustains it.
